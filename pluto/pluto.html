Pluto, the Planet with a Heart!



Pluto was discovered by Clyde Tombaugh in 1930 at the Lowell Observatory in Flagstaff, Arizona. Long regarded as a planet, Pluto was downgraded to a dwarf planet in 2006 during a meeting of the IAU in Prague. The move was controversial, and is even more so now after the New Horizons mission revealed what a complex and amazing world Pluto really is. The current battle pits astronomers versus planetary scientists. Each side considers the criteria of what defines a planet differently.

The Astronomers

  • The object must be in orbit around the Sun.
  • The object must be massive enough to be rounded by its own gravity. More specifically, its own gravity should pull it into a shape defined by hydrostatic equilibrium.
  • It must have cleared the neighborhood around its orbit.

The Planetary Scientists

  • The object need not be in orbit around the Sun. It can be in orbit around another planet, or be a rogue planet.
  • Planet: a sub-stellar mass body that has never undergone nuclear fusion and that has sufficient self-gravitation to assume a spheroidal shape adequately described by a triaxial ellipsoid regardless of its orbital parameters.
  • Planetary scientists point out that by the terms of the new definition, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, and Neptune, all of which share their orbits with asteroids, would be excluded.
